So what is a large language model? This is what you're interacting with any time that you use Sierra. Any time that I type something in the chat window and I press enter, what I'm doing is I'm sending this information to what's called a large language model. Now, language models are not the only type of AI — there are many different types of AI — but this is the most popularized AI in the last few years. And I'd like to provide a really high-level overview of how these models were developed and why they're so effective today.

So language models really came about starting in 2016, but they've of course advanced tremendously here in the last few years, even in the last year. It's really just been amazing to watch the reasoning power increase. What allowed these models to really come into being, at least a key component of it, was the internet. Over the last few decades, as more and more people started posting, writing articles and publishing websites, it essentially created this plethora of training data that companies could then come in and start feeding to what are called neural networks. We won't get into all the technical details of what that means, but suffice to say, through feeding in these massive amounts of textual data — all scraped from the web as well as other curated open source data sources — by feeding it through these models, they were able to start building out general models of how language fits together, how it links — highly sophisticated statistical relations between different words, how language is constructed, and ultimately prediction models of how to continue text.

And that ultimately is what a language model is doing. It's taking some kind of textual input — anything that we upload or put into our chat window — and it's running it through the model based on all its statistical relations and patterns that it has established. It's able to produce the statistically most likely continuation of that text. And that, of course, is our output. So all these models in some form or fashion are designed to continue or to complete the text that you provided as input. And that's why I often tell people, you can almost think of an LLM as a sort of calculator — put text in, it runs its process, and it produces its text output, right?

So that's a high-level overview of large language models. Moving forward, what I'd like to start focusing more on is the kind of information that the model "knows."
