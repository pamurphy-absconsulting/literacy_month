# Day 7 — Tokens and Context Windows

Hi everyone. Welcome back. This is Sierra Literacy Month. We'll continue to break down concepts related to artificial intelligence. And until now, I've been focusing a lot more on just making sure we have a general grasp of what large language models are, the kinds of information they already have, and the kinds of information that we need to provide — either by way of file uploads, prompting, or information that can be retrieved from internal knowledge systems that we at the CoE have curated and made available to the model. Moving forward, I want to start focusing more on what the general processing actually looks like of the information that we provide. And as discussed, special focus or emphasis placed more so on the constraints and certain things to be mindful of to really get the best responses as we can from the model.

So I'm sure you've heard the word tokens thrown around a little bit. I understand there is a lot of new jargon being thrown around as we start integrating AI more and more into our workflow. Tokens you could think of as simple units of measurement related to language. The reason being is that language is just inherently messy — there's quite a bit of variance within it. The word "language," for example, compared to the word "token," are wildly different lengths, different etymology, different structure. And these machines typically need just a more standardized unit of how to measure things and what to process. So that's how we have tokens. You can think of these as simply small chunks of text — oftentimes just words or parts of words. Punctuation is often its own token. Generally about 666 tokens per page. And again, really grasping tokens is not so important, but it is relevant when we start talking about some of the constraints which I'll be getting into here shortly.

Now with tokens, it's interesting — this is how the model is actually processing: token by token. And this is also what it's generating. So when I give it a sentence like "language models break text into smaller tokens before processing it," it'll chunk that into the pieces that we have color-coded here. And then it will embed each of these tokens. Embeddings you can think of as just a really high-dimensional mathematical coordinate — a string of numbers representing where this actual token sits in this high-dimensional space. And this is what allows models to do this sort of semantic grasping of language. Two similar words, for example, may sit in close proximity in this high-dimensional space. And this is what allows the model to understand that these are related, or they're linked, or they're similar in some way. So kind of interesting. These are some made-up embeddings I have here for "language" and "models," just to give you an idea of what these generally look like.

Now when we're submitting information to Sierra — this could be in the way of information we provide in the prompt, files that we've uploaded, or information it's retrieving internally from data sets that we've curated — what it's generally processing is taking that entire conversation history and sifting through that one by one. Now the relevance here in terms of your actual application and using Sierra in your day-to-day has to do with understanding the enormity of the content that it's actually processing on each run. So the way we're actually often times going to get better responses, more accurate responses from the model is by tightening up our focus related to the questions that we're asking. We're feeding in specific context related to this specific problem, and then asking specific, targeted questions on that data set or whatever I've provided.

If I have one thread going — I open up a chat within Sierra and I start talking about this project, then I bring up this other project, then I switch over to this client work, then I start asking about this random area of curiosity — every time, the model is still having to reprocess everything from scratch from the beginning of the conversation. So essentially what I'm doing is I'm introducing a lot of noise, just a lot of extra information that it's having to sift through and navigate to actually grasp what it is that I'm really asking for in this specific iteration and how to best answer that. So this is why we always encourage people to start new chats when they switch topics — it's going to allow the model to have a more focused process pertaining to the specific topic at hand. Focused threads get better answers.

Now lastly, I want to touch on context window. And I know I'm kind of just bringing this in last minute, but this is really important. And this is why tokens are more relevant. Often times you're going to see that a model has a certain context window — 200,000 tokens, 400,000 tokens. The reason I wanted to really help us grasp generally what tokens are is so we do understand the context window, because this is going to be the primary constraint as it relates to processing information. The context window you could think of as the working memory. This is everything the model can process at once. So if I have a conversation going and I just keep it going — switching topics, going on and on, pages and pages and pages — I'm filling up that context window. And at a certain point, the context window is going to be full and the model is not going to be able to process everything within that window. So it's forced to start cutting off previous information. So if I introduce one topic in the beginning of our conversation, meander through, talk about 100 different things, and then ask about that same topic further down in the conversation — it may no longer actually have that information in its context window and thus not be able to provide an answer related to that.

So again, context window is something that I hope we can just start generally being mindful of when we're processing information. Because again, if we fill that out — if we introduce too much information that's not relevant — it is going to have to start cutting out pieces of context, usually from the beginning, in order to run its process. And again, that will affect the results that we receive.

So that's the general idea of tokens and context windows. In the next, I want to talk about hallucinations — this is just when models start making up information. We'll dive into that. And as always, really appreciate your time and attention — until then.
