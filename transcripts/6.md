What they "know" — what they already "have" — is based entirely on the training data provided, oftentimes scraped from the web and curated through various open source repositories. And context being the specific information that we have to provide, such as client data, project information, that we can then provide to the model for it to reason over and offer more contextually rich, relevant responses related to our work.

Now the topic of — how do you make the model more aware of the kinds of information that we're working on? Because I always tell people that models are really only as useful as the information they have access to or the information they were trained on. So of course we can control a lot of this by way of context, but for larger enterprise contexts, there are a couple different approaches. Oftentimes you'll hear of what's called fine-tuning — oftentimes using an open source model and then curating a data set specific to the company or the project that we're working on, and feeding that in and training it so it "knows" about our company, about this project. Now this is a fine approach, but we're still left with a static model — which is to say it's only going to have the information based on the data that it was trained on and it will not be dynamic and shifting. And within an enterprise setting, a lot of the information that we're working on is dynamic. We bring on new clients, we take on new projects, we hire new employees. All the information is constantly shifting.

And for that reason, here at the CoE as well as various other enterprises, we've taken more of what you can almost think of as a knowledge architecture approach — where you're building out and configuring data that the model can then pull from, bring into its context, and reason over.
