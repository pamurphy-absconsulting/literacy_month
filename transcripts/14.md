Hi everyone. Welcome back to Sierra Literacy Month. In this one I want to focus on what are called memories.

This is a feature we have built out within Sierra that allows you to store key pieces of information that are always made available to the model. As we discussed, the models that we're working with are static. They're not learning from our conversations, they're not bringing in any kind of new information. And ultimately, the information that the model has access to — the information that we provide — is what determines its usefulness to us.

As we've discussed at length, memories allow us to set key pieces of information once, so we're not having to continuously bring the model back up to speed or repeatedly bring that information into the context for it to consider in its output.

Memories you can think of simply as persistent context — information that's saved and always brought into the context window. This all happens in the background, so you're not going to necessarily see it inside your chat. It's just included in the background and sent to the model for processing. What it does is ground the model's output and allow it to provide more contextually rich, more tailored responses that take this key background information into account.

So let's talk more specifically on the kind of information we would want to save and store in memories. This needs to be information that's not necessarily shifting week to week. You do have flexibility to delete memories you've stored — we'll talk more about that shortly. But as a general rule of thumb, I want to store information that's not shifting with time.

This could be my role, my specialization, my capabilities. It could also be broader information about my operational context — the industry I'm in, the region, the team I work with, general organizational structure. I could also include preferences: my preferred writing style, the output format I prefer from the model. For example: "Where possible, list this in bullets. Where possible, build information out as a table." And we could go on and on with different options for what to store as a memory.

Again, as a general rule of thumb, think of this as the background information about you — your role, the kind of work that you do — to provide more relevant context so you don't have to repeatedly inform the model or bring it back into your context window with every new chat.

As far as how you set these, it's relatively straightforward. It's dynamic. Anytime you're in a chat, you can simply instruct Sierra to remember something. I could be moving through a chat and then instruct Sierra: "Hey, remember a few of the key points that we've discussed here." It can auto-generate a summary and wait for your approval before saving that to memory.

A better approach that I would typically recommend is to do this more methodically and intentionally. Simply open up a new chat and start moving through a list of things you want Sierra to know — one by one. "Hey Sierra, I work in this space, I do this, this is my industry, this is my region — remember this." And just move through. Then I can always go back into my user settings, click on memories, and see everything that's been saved. I can delete ones that are no longer relevant, or clear out all memories and start fresh.

So in the next, we'll start working more into what are called workspaces — this is where we're really tying in a lot of the information that we've covered today. As always, I really appreciate your time and attention.
