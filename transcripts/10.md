# Day 10 — Structuring Complex Tasks

Hi everyone, welcome back. This is Sierra Literacy Month, where we'll continue to break down the best ways to leverage Sierra. In the last one, I focused more on prompting — just giving more explicit instruction to the model for processing. I want to continue to elaborate on that, but focus more on the way in which we may approach more complex problems that are going to require more multi-step processing.

So just to begin, I want to emphasize that at the end of the day, you really are the subject matter expert here. It's best to think of yourself more as an architect, and then have Sierra execute on specific instruction that you've provided — as opposed to feeding in a lot of information and then asking Sierra to just figure it all out. The reality is that the models, as capable as they are, still lack a lot of the big-picture thinking and simply cannot grasp the full architecture of your problem without your instruction. Also, there's no way for anyone or anything to understand your processes, your project, your systems better than you — you actually live those, and you can apply your own professional judgment. So the job often becomes more one of ensuring that you've provided the necessary information to the model that it couldn't really understand on its own — that you know best — and then communicating the steps that you would like for it to take.

So let's work through one more simple example that will give us a better idea of what this general multi-step process may look like. Let's assume I have a lot of survey data and I want to extract the key findings, then rank those by severity and client impact, and ultimately deliver an executive summary to my client covering the top three.

Now the mistake that we often make is essentially leading with that third step. We load in our data and we say "write a general summary for this client with the top three findings." Sierra can do that often times, but it's going to stumble in certain places because we've sort of overwhelmed it. And we're ultimately always going to get better results if we really focus it — processing task by task by task.

So the way I would approach this would be loading up all the data and first instructing it to pull out all the key findings. This also gives me a good opportunity to really review what we're working with — these are all the findings that were pulled out — and then I can iterate upon that. I could look at those results and give it further instruction: it also needs to consider this, it needs to add in this. I may do that back and forth a few times until I have all the key findings compiled in a way that I'm satisfied with. Then I would instruct Sierra to rank these by severity and client impact — again, another opportunity for me to look at the output, review it, iterate as necessary to ensure I have those rankings the way I want them. And then I would instruct it to write the executive summary for a non-technical client covering the top three. So I've really just held its hand and walked it through step by step — not too different from the way I myself would approach the same problem.

Lastly, I just want to touch on a means of developing or storing prompts for more repeated workflows. Let's say I worked through one of these more multi-step problems and it did require a fair amount of iteration on my part — Sierra doesn't just nail it the first time, there's a lot of back and forth, I really have to dial it in and walk it through. Eventually I do get to the exact output that I want. What I'll do, if I think I'll be doing a similar type of problem in the future, is instruct Sierra to go through the entire conversation history and build out a robust prompt that really outlines all the steps that we took — that I could then apply to the same kind of problem, but maybe in a slightly different domain, or for a different project or client. Then I can save that, and it's going to help kick-start my workflow in the future and really start expediting the task that I'm working on.

So as always, I really appreciate your time and attention. Moving forward, I want to start really diving more into what's called context engineering — which is really just a fancy term for simply ensuring and building out the general structure of all the information that we want Sierra to have access to. Thanks again — until next time.
