So this week, a lot of the focus has been more on just generally gaining a general grasp of the kind of information that these models have baked in from the training data, as well as the kind of information that you would need to provide by way of context — again, this is the information that you put into the prompt window, the files that you upload. And recently, in the last one, I was focusing more on generally how it is processing information — working token by token, that live in these high-dimensional mathematical representations of language and space.

And what I want to focus on here today is hallucinations — which is really where a model starts generating text that's not grounded in any truth. It's simply made-up information. And oftentimes where it happens is within the gaps.

First, I want to just define hallucination. This is not an official definition — this is just something we can share in terms of our understanding: a confident, fluent output not grounded in truth. So if we understand that these models are ultimately purposed to generate a continuation of text based on the inputs they've received as well as all the statistical relations they've established from training data — the model takes in the information provided, it reasons over that based on the patterns and general statistical relationships it has established, and it starts generating token by token, a continuation and ultimately a completion of the information you provide.

Now, when you work with a model and there's insufficient context — maybe it just simply doesn't have the information, or you haven't provided the information, or as we discussed in the last one, you've filled out the context window too much so it's cutting out some of that key information you provided earlier in the conversation — the model will still continue to generate text. It will still attempt to answer based on the input that you provided. And what it's going to work off of is its general modeling of information — how ideas, and more specifically how words and tokens relate to one another. So if I'm asking questions on a specific area of focus that the model doesn't actually have all the information for within its context window, it will still continue to start answering in some way, just based off of rough models.

So that's why a lot of our focus is on helping to better fill those gaps — building out more internal knowledge systems that it can pull from, where it's actually pulling in real data, vetted information, so it's generating responses absolutely grounded in truth. But that's also why it's much your responsibility to ensure that you're providing it with the information it needs, and then, based on the outputs that you're receiving, verifying that those are in fact true.

So in the next, we're going to start really talking about prompting — really giving good instruction to the models and how to leverage these tools moving forward at ABS. But I really wanted to spend more time and focus more on the context, the data ingestion, and general information that these models have — because no amount of prompting will allow the model to answer questions based on information that it doesn't have.
