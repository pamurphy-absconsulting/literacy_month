# Day 4 — What the Model Knows

Hi everyone, welcome back. This is Sierra Literacy Month. We're going to keep breaking down different concepts as it relates to artificial intelligence, with of course special emphasis placed on utilizing Sierra within an ABS context.

Now in the last one, I gave a very high-level overview of large language models. Again, this is what most people are referring to when they talk about AI. These are almost what you could think of as language processors that were trained on massive amounts of text — oftentimes scraped from the web as well as other curated data sets — to give exposure to these models such that it allowed them to start forming these billions of statistical relations between how words relate to one another, so they could reliably predict and complete sentences or ideas or thoughts within a chat interface.

All right, now in this one and moving forward, I want to start giving you a better idea of the kind of information that these models have, or what they "know." And again, I do want to reiterate — these models are not necessarily holding any data. When you ask about a specific topic, it's not necessarily retrieving that from its own database and pulling that back into the chat. You could almost think of it as a sort of compressed intuition. Having been exposed to massive amounts of text, it just starts forming and building out patterns and understanding how different words relate. So a lot of the information that it's been exposed to is ultimately what's going to determine what it "knows."

So in the diagram here — the yellow circle in the middle — I have some general buckets of the kinds of things that you can reliably expect it to know. This could be any kind of public knowledge, general logic, historical events, and generally anything that at one point was open source. As I mentioned, a lot of the data that was used to train these models was scraped from the web. But it's only up until a certain point in time. The models are static. They're not evolving. They don't continually learn and build out more and more information. It's only based on the information that it was exposed to during its training, up until a certain point. And that's what's called the model's training cutoff. I think this is an important idea just to keep in mind — most of the models, at least at the time of this recording, that you access within Sierra have a general cutoff date around late 2024 or early 2025. So if I were to ask it about an event that happened last week or last month, it's not necessarily going to know — unless it pulls that information in from the web, which is a whole other topic we'll talk about in the future.

But again, just please keep in mind that a lot of the information that these models have access to, or that they can reliably provide, is up until a certain point. Now in the blue circles here — this is the kind of information that you cannot expect it to have. Corporate IP being the main one. Again, this is going to be any kind of internal information related to ABS — client work, contracts, past projects, our employees, our general rules. None of this information was ever put on the web. It was never public, and thus was never used in the training of these models. So this is what we would need to provide in the way of context for it to really work on and solve the more specific problems that we're working on within an ABS context.

Lastly, I want to draw your attention to this other blue circle — deep domain knowledge. Now this is shifting. A lot of the model providers have done a fantastic job of building out more curated data sets and feeding these into the models to bolster its knowledge, to just have a better, deeper understanding within a specific domain. But that's still shifting. Often times what you'll notice within Sierra and the models that you pull from within it is it'll have more than a surface-level knowledge, but not necessarily a deep level of domain expertise. And again, that will be shifting. But of course, there are ways to mitigate that by the information that we provide — which is called context. And that's going to be the subject of the next topic — really just helping us differentiate better between the kind of information the model already "knows" and the kind of information that we would need to provide, which again we can refer to as context.

So as always, really appreciate your time and attention. Until next time.
